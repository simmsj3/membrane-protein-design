Introduction: Thinking Like a Protein Designer

The principles you learn today are used to create life-saving medicines and enzymes that break down plastics. This workshop is your first step into this exciting field. Your goal is not to find a single "perfect" answer, but to learn the process of rational design by understanding *why* you are making changes and learning from both your successes and failures.

Your Mission

Last session, you each designed a single 21-amino acid helix. Today, you will work as a team to transform those individual designs into a functional six-helix bundle. Your first task is to collaborate and select the best possible starting sequence. Then, you will optimise it for both protein-protein packing and protein-lipid stability.

Part 1-3: The Theory of Design

Before we begin, review the core principles that will guide your decisions today: the Coiled Coil Principle (our design shortcut), the rules of the Protein-Lipid Interface (being a chemical chameleon), and the thermodynamics of the Protein-Protein Core (managing your stability budget).

Part 4: Your Toolkit - The Virtual Lab

You can't make real proteins in an afternoon, so we use computational tools that act as our "virtual lab." These programs allow us to build a model, test its properties, and get instant feedback on our design decisions.

CCBuilder & PPM Server 3.0

CCBuilder is your "virtual model kit" for building the 3D structure and scoring the packing. The PPM Server is your "virtual cell" for testing how well the structure inserts into a membrane.

Part 5: Team Huddle - Making Your First Design Decision

Science is collaborative. Your first task as a design team is to analyse the different starting helices from your previous work and make a collective, justified decision on the best possible starting point.

Your Huddle Workflow

  1. Compare Your Sequences: Lay out all your team's sequences. Which one best follows the three-zone rule? Who has the best aromatic anchors?
  2. Select or Synthesise: You can either pick the single best sequence from one team member, or create a new "hybrid" sequence by combining the best features from everyone's designs.
  3. Justify Your Choice: Document *why* your chosen sequence is the strongest starting point in your template.

Part 6: A Worked Example - Establishing a Baseline

Before you test your own sequence, let's walk through the full process with a simple but flawed example: a 21-residue all-Alanine helix (AAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAA). This will help you understand the workflow and what the scores mean.

Step 1: Get the Protein:Protein Score from CCBuilder

Click here for Step-by-Step CCBuilder Instructions
  1. Go to the CCBuilder website and click on Build.
  2. In the new window, set the following parameters:
    • Set Oligomeric state to 6.
    • Change Radius to 8.6 Å.
    • Ensure Sequence register is set to ‘a’.
    • Paste the sequence AAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAA into the box.
  3. Press Submit.
  4. Record the BUDE Energy score and download the **PDB file**.

Step 2: Get the Protein:Lipid Score from PPM Server

  1. Go to the PPM Server 3.0 website.
  2. Upload the PDB file you just downloaded from CCBuilder.
  3. Use the default "Mammalian plasma membrane" and press submit.
  4. Record the ΔG of transfer from the results page.

Step 3: Analyse the Results

MetricExample Score for Ala21What does this mean?
BUDE Energy (Packing)~ -190A relatively poor score. Alanine sidechains are small and don't make many favourable van der Waals contacts, so the packing is weak.
ΔG of transfer (Insertion)~ -25 kcal/molA very good score. The helix is extremely hydrophobic, so it strongly prefers to be in the oily membrane rather than water.

Conclusion: The all-Alanine helix has a **happy Membrane Guru** (great insertion score) but a very **unhappy Architect** (poor packing score) and **unhappy Channel Engineer** (no polar channel). Your goal is to design a sequence that gets good scores for *both* metrics while also having a functional channel.

Part 7: Your Team's Design Challenge - The Iterative Rounds

Now it's your turn. Apply the exact same workflow from the worked example to your team's champion sequence. Assign roles, get your baseline scores, and then work together to improve them. Remember to change only one or two residues at a time!

Assign Your Team Roles

The Architect

Your Goal: Maximise bundle stability. Your Focus: The 'a' and 'd' core positions, balancing ΔH and TΔS. Your Score: BUDE Energy in CCBuilder.

The Membrane Guru

Your Goal: Ensure stable insertion. Your Focus: The 'b'/'c'/'f' interface positions, using MemProtMD data. Your Score: ΔG transfer in PPM Server.

The Channel Engineer

Your Goal: Create a hydrophilic channel. Your Focus: The 'e'/'g' positions, ensuring they are polar and uncharged.

Begin Your Iterations

Architect: “Our BUDE score is poor. I suggest changing 'a' and 'd' positions to Leucine to improve packing.”

Channel Engineer: “The central pore is too hydrophobic. I recommend changing the 'g' position to Threonine.”

Membrane Guru: “We need better aromatic anchors. Let's change the 'b' positions in the first and last heptads to Tryptophan.”

Pro-Tip for Iteration

Change only one or two amino acids at a time! If you change many things at once, you won't know which change was responsible for the new score. Good science is about isolating variables.
