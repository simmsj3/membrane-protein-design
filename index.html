Biochemistry Computer-Aided Learning Workshop

Learning Outcomes

  • Annotate a 21-residue sequence into heptads (a–g) and justify residue choices at different positions.
  • Build and score a six-helix bundle in CCBuilder and interpret BUDE energy qualitatively.
  • Predict and rationalise ΔGtransfer trends across three membrane compositions using PPM 3.0.
  • Run hypothesis-driven iterations and present clear reasoning for your design decisions.

Your Mission

Last session, you each designed a single 21-amino acid helix. Today, you will work as a team to transform those individual designs into a functional six-helix bundle. Your first task is to collaborate and select the best possible starting sequence. Then, you will optimise it for both protein-protein packing and protein-lipid stability.

Part 1: The Coiled Coil - A Design Shortcut

The coiled coil is a brilliant "design pattern" that nature uses to build stable structures. By following the heptad repeat (a,b,c,d,e,f,g), we can confidently predict that our helices will assemble correctly, freeing us up to focus on the fine details of the design.

Part 2: The Protein-Lipid Interface - Being a Chemical Chameleon

The outside of a membrane protein must perfectly match three different environments at once: the watery solution, the polar headgroups, and the oily lipid tails. Your goal in this section is to solve this puzzle by creating a single, continuous helix that presents the right "face" to its surroundings at every depth.

Designing the Outside: What Goes Where?

Consult the MemProtMD Amino Acid Statistics page. By analysing the "Interface" layers, you will find that a sophisticated interface helix needs:

  • Aromatic Anchors: Tyrosine (Y) and Tryptophan (W).
  • Snorkeling Residues: Lysine (K) and Arginine (R).

Part 3: The Protein-Protein Core - The Engine Room

This is the "engine room" of your protein, where the forces that hold the entire bundle together are generated. Your goal is to make the Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG) as negative as possible by creating a perfectly packed, energetically favourable core. This requires balancing two key thermodynamic forces.

The Thermodynamics of Packing: A Stability Budget

Stability is governed by ΔG = ΔH - TΔS.

  • Enthalpy (ΔH) is your Income: Good interactions (like H-bonds or van der Waals packing) make ΔH more negative.
  • Entropy (TΔS) is your Expense: Forcing a flexible sidechain into a fixed position costs entropy. The bigger the sidechain, the bigger the cost.

Part 5: Team Huddle - Making Your First Design Decision

Science is collaborative. Your first task as a design team is to analyse the different starting helices from your previous work and make a collective, justified decision on the best possible starting point.

Your Huddle Workflow

  1. Compare Your Sequences: Lay out all your team's sequences. Which one best follows the three-zone rule? Who has the best aromatic anchors?
  2. Select or Synthesise: You can either pick the single best sequence from one team member, or create a new "hybrid" sequence by combining the best features from everyone's designs.
  3. Justify Your Choice: Document *why* your chosen sequence is the strongest starting point in your template.

Part 6: Assign Roles & Begin Iterative Design

Now, assign your team roles. Each member will become an expert responsible for one aspect of the design. The iterative design process is the core of the workshop: form a hypothesis, test it, analyse the results, and repeat.

The Architect

Your Goal: Maximise bundle stability. Your Focus: The 'a' and 'd' core positions, balancing ΔH and TΔS. Your Tool: CCBuilder.

The Membrane Guru

Your Goal: Ensure stable insertion. Your Focus: The 'b'/'c'/'f' interface positions, using MemProtMD data. Your Tool: PPM Server 3.0.

The Channel Engineer

Your Goal: Create a hydrophilic channel. Your Focus: The 'e'/'g' positions, ensuring they are polar and uncharged.

Architect: “Our BUDE score is poor. I suggest changing 'a' and 'd' positions to Leucine to improve packing.”

Channel Engineer: “The central pore is too hydrophobic. I recommend changing the 'g' position to Threonine.”

Membrane Guru: “We need better aromatic anchors. Let's change the 'b' positions in the first and last heptads to Tryptophan.”

Pro-Tip for Iteration

Change only one or two amino acids at a time! If you change many things at once, you won't know which change was responsible for the new score. Good science is about isolating variables.

Part 7: Advanced Analysis - Stress-Testing Your Design

A protein designed for a human cell might not function in a bacterium. The goal of this final step is to "stress-test" your design. By placing your protein in different virtual membranes, you can determine if you've created a robust, universal design or a highly tuned specialist.
