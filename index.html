Mission Briefing: Design a Synthetic Water Channel

Welcome, Biochemists. Your mission, should you choose to accept it, is to design a functional, synthetic membrane protein from scratch. In previous sessions, you've mastered the fundamentals of the protein-lipid interface. Today, you'll take the next leap.

Your team will design a protein composed of a six-transmembrane (6TM) helix bundle. But this isn't just any protein. Your design must feature a central channel, or pore, that is hydrophilic enough to allow a water molecule to pass through its core. This is a fundamental feature of many natural ion channels and transporters.

To succeed, you will need to balance two competing forces:

  1. Favourable Protein-Protein Interactions: The helices must pack together tightly and stably.
  2. Favourable Protein-Lipid Interactions: The entire bundle must want to sit happily within the cell membrane.

You will work in teams of 3-4, with each member taking on a specialist role. Only by combining your unique expertise will you be able to design a protein that meets the challenge. Let's get designing!

Part 1: The Theory - Simplifying Complexity with Coiled Coils

Designing a protein from scratch seems impossibly complex. How do we predict how the helices will arrange themselves? The answer lies in using a simplified design principle: the coiled coil.

A coiled coil is a structural motif in proteins where alpha-helices are coiled together like strands of a rope. This structure is famously stable and, more importantly, predictable. The predictability comes from a repeating pattern of seven amino acids called a heptad repeat, denoted by the letters a, b, c, d, e, f, g.

A helical wheel diagram showing the positions of the heptad repeat.

The 'magic' of the coiled coil lies in the chemical nature of the amino acids at specific positions:

  • Positions 'a' and 'd' (The Core): These positions form the central core where the helices touch. To make the helices stick together through the hydrophobic effect, these positions are typically filled with hydrophobic amino acids (like Leucine, Isoleucine, Valine, or Alanine). This creates a "hydrophobic seam" that zippers the helices together.
  • Positions 'e' and 'g' (The Flanks): These positions are on the edges of the hydrophobic core. They often contain charged or polar amino acids. In our design, they will be critical for creating the water channel.
  • Positions 'b', 'c', and 'f' (The Outside): These positions face the "world" of the lipid bilayer, so these positions must be filled with hydrophobic amino acids to ensure the protein is stable in the membrane.

By using this simple abcdefg pattern, we can rationally design sequences that will reliably fold into a helical bundle, dramatically simplifying our design process.

Part 2: Your Team Roles & The Design Challenge

In your team of 3-4, assign the following specialist roles. Each of you will become an expert in one area.

🏗 The Architect

Your Goal: Get the best possible protein-protein interaction score.

Your Tool: CCBuilder.

Your Focus: The 'a' and 'd' positions. A more negative BUDE Energy score means a more stable bundle. You are responsible for interpreting this score.

🌊 The Membrane Guru

Your Goal: Ensure the protein can insert itself correctly into the cell membrane.

Your Tool: PPM Server.

Your Focus: The 'b', 'c', and 'f' positions. You will analyse the ΔG transfer, hydrophobic thickness, and tilt angle.

💧 The Channel Engineer

Your Goal: Create a hydrophilic channel down the center of the bundle.

Your Tool: Your knowledge of amino acid properties.

Your Focus: The 'e' and 'g' positions. To create a water-friendly channel, you must select small, polar, uncharged amino acids.

Part 3: The Design Workflow - A Worked Example

Let's start with a terrible initial sequence to get a baseline score: AAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAA (27 amino acids, or Ala27).

Step 1: The Architect's Job (CCBuilder Score)

  1. Go to the CCBuilder website.
  2. Click Build.
  3. In the new window:
    • Set Oligomeric state to 6.
    • Set Radius to 8.6 Å.
    • Ensure the sequence register is 'a'.
    • Paste your sequence into the box and press Submit.
  4. Download the PDB file and record the BUDE Energy. For Ala27, the score is -194.9.
What is BUDE Energy? The Birmingham University Docking Engine (BUDE) calculates the interaction energy between the helices. It's a measure of enthalpy (how well things stick together). A more negative score indicates better, more stable packing.

Step 2: The Membrane Guru's Job (PPM Server Score)

  1. Go to the PPM Server website.
  2. Upload the PDB file you downloaded from CCBuilder and press Submit.

You will get an output with several values:

  • ΔG transfer: The Gibbs free energy of transfer. A large negative value (e.g., < -10 kcal/mol) is very good. For Ala27, our score is -24.6 kcal/mol. This is good! The protein is very hydrophobic.
  • Depth/Hydrophobic Thickness: This should ideally match a real cell membrane (~30 Å). Our score is 33 Å, which is reasonable.
  • Tilt Angle: Our score of 35 degrees is quite high, which could indicate imperfect design.

Conclusion: The all-Alanine helix is great at entering the membrane (happy Membrane Guru) but is poorly packed and has no water channel (unhappy Architect and Channel Engineer). Your job is to improve all scores!

Round 1: Baseline Scores

Design your first sequence. Think about the roles of all the abcdefg positions. Get your baseline CCBuilder and PPM server scores. Discuss them as a team.

Rounds 2, 3, and 4: Design, Test, Refine!

In each round, propose a new sequence. Each team member should advocate for changes based on their role:

Architect: "Our BUDE score is poor. I suggest we change the 'a' and 'd' positions from Alanine to Leucine to improve the packing."

Channel Engineer: "The core is still too hydrophobic. I recommend changing the 'g' position to Threonine to add a hydroxyl group."

Membrane Guru: "Our ΔG score is getting less negative. Let's ensure the 'b', 'c', and 'f' positions remain strongly hydrophobic."

Part 1: The Theory - Simplifying Complexity with Coiled Coils

Designing a protein from scratch seems impossibly complex. How do we predict how the helices will arrange themselves? The answer lies in using a simplified design principle: the coiled coil.

A coiled coil is a structural motif where alpha-helices coil together like strands of a rope. The structure's predictability comes from a repeating pattern of seven amino acids called a heptad repeat, denoted by the letters a, b, c, d, e, f, g.

The 'magic' of the coiled coil lies in the chemical nature of the amino acids at specific positions:

  • Positions 'a' and 'd' (The Core): These positions form the central core where the helices touch. To make the helices stick together through the hydrophobic effect, these positions are typically filled with hydrophobic amino acids (like Leucine, Isoleucine, Valine).
  • Positions 'e' and 'g' (The Flanks): These positions are on the edges of the hydrophobic core. In our design, they will face the central channel and must be polar to create a water-friendly pore.
  • Positions 'b', 'c', and 'f' (The Outside): These positions face the "world" of the lipid bilayer, so these positions must be filled with hydrophobic amino acids to ensure the protein is stable in the membrane.

By using this simple abcdefg pattern, we can rationally design sequences that will reliably fold into our target hexameric bundle.

Designing the Inside: The Thermodynamics of Packing

The stability of your protein bundle is governed by the Gibbs free energy equation: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS. To make a stable protein (very negative ΔG), we need favourable interactions (negative ΔH) and minimal loss of randomness (small negative or positive ΔS).

Entropy (TΔS): The Cost of Packing Sidechains

Entropy is about disorder. A long, flexible sidechain like Leucine (L) has many possible conformations when unfolded. Forcing it into one position inside a protein core causes a large loss of entropy. This is an entropic penalty that makes ΔG less negative.

A small sidechain like Alanine (A) has very little flexibility to begin with, so the entropic penalty for packing it is tiny. This is entropically favourable.

Enthalpy (ΔH): The Right Interactions in the Right Place

Enthalpy is about the energy of bonds (van der Waals, H-bonds). But the *value* of a bond depends on its environment!

  • In the Hydrophobic Core (Heptad 2): This environment is completely water-free. A hydrogen bond between two polar residues (e.g., Serine-Serine) is **extremely powerful** here because there's no water to compete with it. This gives a huge negative ΔH. However, burying an unsatisfied polar group is catastrophic. It's a high-risk, high-reward strategy.
  • At the Interface (Heptads 1 & 3): This environment is messy and water-accessible. A specific H-bond is weak here, as water can make the same bond just as easily. In this chaotic zone, the simple, reliable van der Waals packing of hydrophobic residues (like Valine or Alanine) at the a and d positions is often a more robust way to get a favourable ΔH.

Your challenge is to balance these competing forces. Do you use large hydrophobic residues (great ΔH, terrible TΔS), small hydrophobic residues (poor ΔH, great TΔS), or risky polar residues (potentially amazing ΔH)? The best core is often a clever mix.
